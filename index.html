a curriculum
for a year.

No school, no syllabus, no one checking in. Just me deciding that a gap year could mean something — that nine months of genuine curiosity, documented honestly, was worth more than nine months of waiting to start.

This is that record. One topic per month, one project at the end. Everything made by hand, in public.

On taking a gap year

A year with
no one to
answer to
but myself.

And that turned out to be the hardest part.

Why a gap year

I didn't take a gap year because I was lost. I took it because I wanted to actually choose what comes next — not just accept the first door that opened. That meant buying myself time to grow, to try things, to be uncomfortable entirely on my own terms.

What nobody tells you

When there's no structure, you become the structure. No teacher, no deadline, no peer pressure to show up. I had to be strict with myself in a way school never required. Showing up for yourself, consistently, when no one is watching — that's the real education.

What I actually did

Freelancing — design, content, websites. Writing essays every Sunday on Substack, whether or not anyone reads them. Building a following on social media from scratch — 324 followers with no team, no budget, just consistency. And the Brain Diet: nine months of deliberate, self-directed learning, one field at a time.

What I grew into

Someone who doesn't wait to be assigned a task. Someone who's failed publicly enough to not be afraid of it. Someone who knows that growth, when it's real, is slow and quiet and rarely looks impressive while it's happening.
